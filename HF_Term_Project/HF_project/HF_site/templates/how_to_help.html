How YOU Can Help End Homelessness


Save this resource

While the causes and solutions to homelessness are extemely complex, there is still so much that an individual or community can do to help. Regardless of your skills, interests, age, or resources, there are ways that you can make a difference for those who are experiencing homelessness. Volunteer work, advocacy efforts, contributions, and continued self-education are all vital as we work towards finding a solution. Some suggestions are listed below.


Volunteer

Volunteering your time to help directly is one of the best ways to learn about homelessness and to help the immediate needs of those who are experiencing homelessness. There is always a lot of available "behind the scenes" work to be done at homeless shelters, such as filing, sorting clothes, helping to prepare meals, etc. Take into consideration what you enjoy and what you think you may be best at, whether that is working with individuals or groups, with men, women, or children, cooking, and so on. The next step is to find and call a few places near you, ask what they need help with, and arrange a time to visit.

Here are some ways that you may be able to help:

  • Work at a shelter. Take some time out of your day to help a nearby shelter with clerical work, such as answering phones, typing, filing, or sorting mail. You can also serve food, wash dishes, or sort and distribute clothes.
  • Help build or fix up houses or shelters. Check with your local public housing authority or find the nearest chapter of Habitat for Humanity by calling 800-422-4828 or by visiting https://www.habitat.org/
  • Organize an event at a shelter. Plan a program such as a bingo or chess night, an open mic poetry, storytelling, or more at a homeless shelter.

Advocate

Advocacy is vital to creating the systemic changes needed to end homelessness in America. Advocacy means working with people who are experiencing homelessness in order to bring about positive changes in policies and programs on the local, state, and federal levels. This involves working with various sectors of the community, such as city offices, members of Congress, and direct service providers.

Here are some ways that you may be able to help:

  • Get connected to a coalition. Volunteer at your local, state, or national housing or homeless advocacy coalition, or make a financial contribution to support their work.
  • Follow local politics. Attend neighborhood and public meetings and speak up in favor of low-income housing, group homes, shelters, and homelessness prevention programs.
  • Register people experiencing homelessness to vote. The "You Don't Need A Home to Vote" nonpartisan voter registration/education/get-out-to-vote campaign occurs nationwide each election cycle. The National Homeless and Low-Income Voter Registration Week occurs every year during the last week of September, and provides a week of registration events aimed specifically at registering voters who are unhoused.
  • Become more aware of your language. People who are experiencing homelessness are still people and should be respected and treated as such. Try to minimize vocabulary that refers to people who are experiencing homelessness in derogatory ways. Expressions such as "people experiencing homelessness" rather than labels like "bum," "transient," or even "the homeless," reminds us that people who are in such situations are still people first - just people who are going through something incredibly difficult in their lives.

Contribute

While volunteer work and advocacy are essential, material assistance is also very necessary. Escaping homelessness is a very long and tedious process, and in the meantime, people experiencing homelessness and the programs that serve them need help everyday.

Items and services that may be needed near you:

  • Clothing. Lack of clean and well-fitting clothing and shoes causes great hardship against the elements, and it hurts one's self-image and one's chance to get ahead in life. People experiencing homelessness need to travel light, and have very few, if any at all, opportunities to safely store or clean what they can't carry. Poorly dressed people have less of a chance of getting jobs and success. Consider donating your clean clothes to those who can use them. Find your local shelter to see what clothing items they may need most before starting a clothing drive or similar.
  • Household goods or other items. Kitchen utensils, furniture, toys, games, stuffed animals, etc. are always welcomed at shelters.
  • "Survival kits." Create and distribute kits that include necessities, such as cups, utensils, soap, shampoo, toothpaste, toothbrushes, pots, pans, etc. During the colder months, consider organizing drives for blankets, coats, hats, socks, and the like.
